Lab 3: "I came, I saw, I soldered"

Description

In this lab I learned how to solder both stranded and solid-core wires to a Protoboard.

Materials Used

  • Protoboard
  • Stranded Wire
  • Solid-core Wire
  • Soldering Iron
  • Lead-free, rosin core Solder Wire
  • Fume Extractor
  • Brass Wire Pad
  • Soldering Helping Hands
  • Solder Sucker Desoldering Pump
  • Solder Wick
  • Wire Stripper with Wire Cutter
  • Safety Glasses (Regular glasses in my case)

Procedure

Part 1: Soldering Setup and Safety

Since I was unable to do the lab in the BTU lab, I created my own soldering station at home. First I laid down a mat so no solder would get on the table. Next I set up the fume extractor to suck up any fumes that come from the soldering iron. The soldering iron was then set up with the brass pad for cleaning the tip of the soldering iron.

When working with a soldering iron it is really important to use proper safety. I always wash my hands both before and after working since residue will get on your hands. I wore my glasses to protect my eyes from any splatter that might occur. I also wore a mask while working with the iron so as to not inhale any toxic fumes and protect my face. I made sure that the area was well lit and had lots of space for the lab.

Part 2: Soldering Wires to Protoboard

To begin I started with tinning and cleaning the tip of my iron. I used a small amount of solder and the brass pad to clean the tip of the iron.

Now I began to solder the wires to the board. First I had to cut the red, stranded wire to be four inches. I stripped about 2 millimeters off of each end of the wire. I started in the corner of the board with one end and then went four spaces over to attach the other end of the wire. Next I did the same for two, four inch white, solid-core wires in different rows. Below is a picture of all three wires connected.

I found that soldering the stranded wire to the Protoboard was much easier than the solid-core. The stranded wire has more surface area that would attach to the Protoboard. The solid-core wire did not like to hold in the solder as well which made it hard to get a good joint.

Part 3: Bridge of Solder

For this part of the lab I created three different traces that connected the wires together. This takes two different joints and connects them together on the Protoboard. Below is a picture of the traces that I created.

Part 4: Splicing Wires

In this part of the lab I practiced splicing wires together. First I cut the stranded wire into four, two inch pieces. Then I used the wire stripper to strip about 2 millimeters off of each end of the wires. Next, I used the helping hands and put two wires in an alligator clip. I put a little bit of solder on one side of each of the wires. Then I positioned them so that the sides with the solder were touching. When I placed the soldering iron on the joint the solder from each wire melted together. I repeated this process for attaching the other two wires. Lab 1: Simple Circuits

Description

For this lab I explored the concepts of electrical components in series and in parallel. This was also an introductory lab to using a breadboard to connect electrical components. I created a circuit that used resistors, and LED and button to light up the LEDs.

Materials Used

For this lab I used the following materials from my Arduino Uno Starter Kit:

  • Power Supply Module
  • Button
  • 830 Tie-Points Breadboard
  • Breadboard Jumper Wires
  • 9V Battery with Snap-on Connector Clip
  • 100 ohm Resistors
  • LED
  • Multimeter

Procedure

  1. The first step in this lab was to set up power to the breadboard. I attached the power supply module to the breadboard so that one column of the board would be 5 volts and the other side would be 3.3 volts. Below are pictures of the multimeter reading out 5 volts and on the other side 3.3 volts:

  2. Next I created a simple circuit that consisted of a 9 volt battery, a 100 ohm resistor, a blue LED and wires to connect them. For this circuit I used the 3.3V side of the breadboard.

  3. Next I created the same simple circuit but used the 5 volt source. Since the voltage was higher I needed to use a different resistor to make sure the current was within range. I found that the resistor needs to be at least 167 ohms so I just used two 100 ohm resistors in series with each other.

  4. After this I incorporated a momentary switch into the circuit in series with the LED and resistor. When adding the button the LED will only turn on when the button is pressed. This is because the button creates a break in the circuit when not pressed, this does not allow current to flow. When the button is pressed the circuit is connected and current can flow freely.

  5. For the extra credit assignment we had to get the button to work in reverse: the light turned off when the button was pressed. To do this I made the button and LED in parallel with each other. The 100 ohm resistor is in series with each loop in the parallel circuit so that no matter which direction current flows it has to go through the resistor. The reason that the button works opposite from when it was in series is because of the way current flows through parallel circuits. The LED can be thought of as a very small resistor in a circuit. The button, when pressed, can be thought of as a wire with negligible resistance. When current gets to the junction where it either goes to the LED (acting like a resistor) or through the button (acting like a wire) it chooses the “path of least resistance” and the current goes through the button instead of the branch with the LED. When the button is not pressed then that loop is a broken circuit and no current can flow through that branch causing all of it to go to the LED.
